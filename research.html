Research

My research focuses on experimental cosmology, with an emphasis on cryogenic instrumentation and observational analysis. I have also worked on post‑processing methods for high‑contrast imaging of directly imaged exoplanets. Use the tabs below to switch between areas.

Terahertz Intensity Mapper (TIM) & SPIDER

Observational Cosmology & Particle Astrophysics Lab (UIUC)  •  Advisor: Prof. Jeffrey Filippini

At the Observational Cosmology & Particle Astrophysics Lab, I work on cryogenic instrumentation and analysis for balloon‑borne cosmological telescopes. My primary hardware focus is the Terahertz Intensity Mapper (TIM), where I help with the main cryostat integration by developing lab readout, working on the mechanical assembly and cooldown operations of the cryostat. I characterized terahertz absorbers and a terahertz reflective attenuator using a Toptica Terascan 1550 frequency‑selectable laser.

I engineered a blackbody paddle (macro‑bolometer) to probe infrared loading and worked on developing ultra‑thin UHMWPE vacuum windows optimized for mechanical robustness. I also contributed to seeing the optical response of kinetic inductance detector (KID) arrays during instrument first light.

For the SPIDER II CMB balloon experiment, I developed an astrometry package to track Starlink satellite constellations and identify transient signatures in detector timestreams. The tool develops functionality to cross‑correlate satellite positions with CMB data (in HEALPix coordinates) to understand and mitigate satellite interference.

Caltech SURF — Observational Cosmology Group

Caltech  •  June 2025 – Aug 2025  •  Advisors: late Dr. Kenny Lau, Prof. James Bock

As part of the Observational Cosmology Group at Caltech, I commissioned a 100 mK adiabatic demagnetization refrigerator (ADR) testbed for BICEP and TIME detectors. I installed and optimized thermal connections in the cryostat to achieve a 48 hour hold time, and integrated multiplexed TES detector readout.

I helped evaluate new superconducting readout flex cables for the TIME experiment, comparing residual resistances and transition reliability with the existing cables. This work informed the manufacturing recipe for on‑site deployable cables.

Caltech SURF — Exoplanet Technology Lab

Caltech  •  June 2024 – Nov 2025  •  Advisors: Dr. Yinzi Xin, Dr. Nemanja Jovanovic, Prof. Dimitri Mawet

In the Exoplanet Technology Lab at Caltech, I worked on high‑contrast imaging using a photonic lantern nuller (PLN) to directly image exoplanets. My work focused on post‑processing algorithms to enable spatial planet detectability in noisy, rotationally non‑invariant data.

I numerically characterized this for the PLN instrument, identified limitations by the planet self-subtraction, and implemented cost‑function optimization to localize planets. This effort culminated in a co‑authored paper in JATIS and a first‑author manuscript on angular‑differential post‑processing methods for PLN data, currently submitted to JATIS (Preprint Here).
